Future State Analysis

Criminal and Anti-Social Use of E-Mobility in the UK

2024 Analysis NPCC Report Predictive Analysis

1. Introduction

The National Police Chiefs' Council (NPCC) coordinates policing strategies across the UK to ensure effective crime prevention and public safety. NPCC has expressed concerns on the increasing crime rate of e-mobility (electric bike, electric scooter, and electric motorcycle) in the UK. The initial current state analysis conducted revealed the systemic challenges of NPCC, which include:

Increasing surge of e-mobility crimes across UK.

Unavailability of a centralized Database

Unstandardized and inconsistent reporting of e-mobility crimes across the whole police force.

Illegal purchase of e-mobiles from the grey market

Additionally, the analysis of e-mobility crime between 2021 and 2023 across major cities revealed the trends of the offences, the crime hotspots, and the most used e-mobility devices. This future state design aims to present a strategic, data-driven approach that aligns with national policing goals, leverages digital and location-based tools to reduce crime, and ultimately enhances public safety. In response to the current challenges, the proposed future state outlines a comprehensive plan that includes increasing public awareness on the lawful use of e-mobility devices, expanding police patrols during high-risk periods, and delivering consistent training for officers to standardize reporting procedures. It also advocates for implementing an integrated, real-time crime reporting platform and introducing clear legal frameworks. Together, these measures are intended to strengthen collaboration between the public and police, improve the quality of reported data, and ensure greater accountability from users, retailers, and manufacturers within the e-mobility sector.

Geospatial Analysis

Key Findings

E-mobility crime is heavily concentrated in urban centers, particularly in the West and East Midlands, with high-density clusters around Birmingham, Wolverhampton, Derby, Nottingham, and Leicester. Interactive mapping shows:

  • Leicester: A key hotspot, especially in areas like Newfoundpool, Belgrave, and Braunstone, which align with high-traffic zones and urban amenities.
  • Broader Urban Coverage: Extends to Manchester, Sheffield, and Liverpool, although with lower density.
  • Suburban Zones: Moderate activity near major transport routes (e.g., Wigston, Syston, A50, A47, M69, M1, M62).
  • Rural Areas: Few incidents reported, likely due to low usage or underreporting.

Key Insight: Crime density correlates strongly with population and mobility infrastructure. These findings justify targeted interventions such as patrol concentration, lighting upgrades, and community education in high-risk areas.

SLR on the Proposed Solution

I. Smart Crime Reporting Platforms

Smartphone and web-based crime reporting tools with real-time geolocation are increasingly utilized in UK policing. Zhang, Nelson, and Mulley (2024) demonstrate that location-enabled apps enhance public engagement and data accuracy, as evidenced in e-scooter trials. Police dashboards further boost efficiency in high-incident urban areas.

Key Point: Integrating public-facing apps with centralized police dashboards improves data quality, streamlines reporting, and builds user trust.

II. Licensing and Registration of E-Mobility Devices

Greenwood-Reeves (2022) emphasizes that clear regulations, particularly concerning ownership and traceability, are vital for gaining public trust in e-mobility systems. In London’s shared e-scooter trials, digital registration was implemented to reduce misuse by anonymous users.

Key Point: Licensing systems strengthen accountability and deter the criminal use of unregistered devices.

III. Training (Police and Public) and Technology Adoption

Witzell et al. (2022) highlight that community training is essential for promoting responsible use in UK mobility innovation pilots. Schrader et al. (2024) add that officer training enhances the effective use of digital tools and geospatial dashboards.

Key Point: Educating both the public and police facilitates smoother adoption of new technologies.

IV. GPS Integration and Security

Shukla (2025) finds that built-in GPS in UK e-scooters serves a dual purpose—deterring theft and aiding in the recovery of stolen units. The research supports regulatory moves that require manufacturers to include tracking features in retail models.

Key Point: Real-time GPS tracking strengthens theft prevention and enhances law enforcement response.

V. Retailer, Manufacturer & Distributor Regulation

Joshi et al. (2023) identify unregulated sales channels as a primary source of inconsistent safety standards in shared mobility. Martin (2020) corroborates this by showing that retailer registration and inspections improve public safety outcomes.

Key Point: Strengthening retail oversight curbs the spread of unregistered and untracked devices.

VI. Geospatial Crime Monitoring and Policing Efficiency

Martínez (2021) demonstrates that visual dashboards and IoT integration enhance real-time decision-making in public safety. UK councils are increasingly implementing these tools to improve incident response and monitor reporting effectiveness.

Key Point: Geospatial dashboards enable intelligence-led policing and ongoing performance assessment of local forces.

VII. Manufacturer Collaboration and Enforcement

Smith and Coxhead (2024) examine joint efforts between e-mobile manufacturers and UK law enforcement to standardize serial registration and enforce local safety regulations. Their study documents a pilot initiative targeting gang-related misuse of illegal e-scooters in theft and delivery crimes.

Key Point: Collaboration with manufacturers strengthens enforcement and addresses loopholes in unregulated imports.

VIII. Improved Lighting and CCTV Deployment

In a UK criminological study, Baillie (2019) demonstrated that the use of CCTV systems alongside well-planned lighting significantly reduces incidents of property and vehicle-related crime. These preventative measures proved especially effective when supported by mobile surveillance units deployed along busy e-mobility routes.

Key Point: Integrating urban lighting and surveillance strategies helps lower crime concentrations in areas with high mobility risk.

3. Research Methods

a. Mixed Method Research

This study employed a mixed methods design, integrating both quantitative and qualitative analytical techniques to provide a comprehensive understanding of the research problem.

Data Acquisition

The dataset was provided by the client, the National Police Chiefs' Council (NPCC), and shared via the University of Hertfordshire’s institutional email.

Quantitative Analysis

The quantitative component focused on crime data analysis using various computational and statistical tools, including Microsoft Excel, Power BI, Power Query, DAX, and Python. The following methods were applied:

  • Trend Analysis
  • Geospatial Analysis
  • Clustering Techniques
  • Time Series Analysis and Heatmap Visualisations
  • Systematic Literature Review (SLR)

Qualitative Analysis

An initial interview was conducted to capture insights and establish a baseline for the current state analysis. This helped contextualize quantitative findings and refine the research focus.

5. Conclusion

The proposed future state delivers:

  • Enhanced quality and granularity of crime reporting
  • Enforceable and transparent e-mobility regulations
  • Control over grey market distribution, fostering public trust
  • Strong alignment with national digital policy goals

This strategy offers a scalable, data-driven framework for reducing e-mobility crime across the UK. Grounded in advanced analytics and collaboration with stakeholders, it supports a shift from reactive to proactive policing. By utilizing digital infrastructure, structured regulation, and public-private partnerships, it empowers the police to anticipate and effectively respond to urban crime trends.
