AND ANTI-SOCIAL USE OF E-MOBILITY IN UK

1.0 Introduction

The National Police Chiefs' Council (NPCC) coordinates policing strategies across the UK to ensure effective crime prevention and public safety. One of its key focus areas is addressing the rise of e-mobility crime, encompassing e-bikes, e-scooters, and e-motorcycles. Currently, e-mobility crime has rapidly increased in the UK, driven by the widespread adoption of e-scooters, e-bikes, and e-motorcycles, without sufficient legal frameworks to regulate their use. Traditional vehicle crimes have declined by 15% between 2021 and 2023 due to improved anti-theft technologies and public awareness (Reed et al., 2024). In contrast, e-mobility crimes and anti-social behavior—including theft, drug transportation, and reckless riding—have surged by 45% during the same period (Zhang et al., 2024). The absence of mandatory registration and effective tracking systems presents significant challenges for law enforcement. Additionally, public confusion regarding legal requirements and inconsistent enforcement practices hinder the effective management of e-mobility crime. This situation demands strategic interventions focused on legislation, enforcement training, public awareness, and data integration. Furthermore, the scope of this analysis includes:

  • Evaluation of the current state of e-mobility-related crimes
  • Identify key challenges.
  • Comprehensive overview of e-mobility crime trends across major UK cities from 2021 to 2023.
  • Comparative analysis of e-mobility-related crimes with traditional vehicle crimes and identifying key pressure points, pain points, and gaps to enhance crime prevention.
  • This analysis would leverage academic research, governmental reports, and crime data analysis to present a holistic view of e-mobility crime in the UK.
  • This report also incorporates an analysis of the SARA model (Scanning, Analysis, Response, and Assessment), as introduced by the public safety team at AWS during a demonstration meeting.

The SARA model serves as a structured problem-solving framework that targets the reduction of e-mobility crimes through a systematic and data-driven approach. It allows police forces to better manage crime trends and develop tailored interventions (College of Policing, 2021).

Analysis

Definitions

There are various definitions of crime, e-bikes, e-motorcycles, and e-scooters; however, for this report, the definition will be restricted to the one provided by the UK government and referenced in the journals below.

Crime (UK Law): An act or omission that constitutes an offense and is punishable by law (Ministry of Justice, 1967).

E-Bikes (Electrically Assisted Pedal Cycles - EAPCs): Bicycles equipped with an electric motor that assists pedaling, with a maximum power output of 250 watts and a speed limit of 15.5 mph. Riders must be at least 14 years old, and the bike does not require registration or insurance (Doyle, Gilbert & Morgan, 2020).

E-Scooters: Stand-up scooters powered by an electric motor, capable of speeds up to 15.5 mph, and generally not allowed on public roads without a government trial permit. Private e-scooters are illegal on public highways and can only be used on private land with permission (Guy et al., 2024).

E-Motorcycles: Electric motorcycles are treated as conventional motorcycles under UK law, requiring registration, licensing, insurance, and compliance with road regulations (Haq, 2020).

The SARA Model Analysis

Scanning
The scanning phase of the SARA model identifies persistent crime issues and their implications for public safety. During the AWS assembly, the team demonstrated how data analytics can integrate with predictive policing to identify hotspots for e-mobility offenses, using crime statistics and community feedback for targeted crime reduction (College of Policing, 2021). A notable example is Operation Pedal in London, which identified hotspots for e-scooter theft, resulting in a 35% decrease in incidents over six months (Smith & Coxhead, 2024).
Analysis
In the analysis phase, the focus shifts to understanding the root causes of e-mobility crime by examining contributing factors. The AWS team utilized methods such as geospatial mapping and crime pattern analysis to identify high-risk areas (Ratcliffe, 2021). Integrating data from various sources, including police records and public complaints, enhances the accuracy of strategic responses (Tilley, 2020).
Response
The response phase involves implementing strategies to address identified issues. Key suggestions included:
  • Enhanced Surveillance: Deploying CCTV and AI monitoring in hotspots.
  • Community Collaboration: Educating the public on lawful e-mobility use.
  • Data Sharing: Strengthening inter-agency collaboration for better tracking.
  • Technology Integration: Using GPS and RFID (Radio Frequency Identification) for e-scooter theft deterrence.
  • Predictive policing algorithms can help forecast crime trends, enabling proactive law enforcement (Weisburd et al., 2021).
Assessment
The assessment phase evaluates the effectiveness of interventions by measuring crime rate reductions and community satisfaction. Ongoing assessment ensures adaptive strategies for changing criminal activities (College of Policing, 2021). Effectiveness of the SARA Model
The SARA model is validated as an effective crime prevention strategy, reducing crime rates by up to 60% when applied consistently with proper data integration (Ratcliffe, 2021). Its comprehensive framework ensures a systematic approach to policing.
Limitations of the SARA Model
The SARA model has limitations, including:
  • Data Dependency: Relies on accurate data collection (Ratcliffe, 2021).
  • Implementation Variability: Inconsistent application reduces effectiveness (Tilley, 2020).
  • Resource Intensive: Requires significant resources for execution (Weisburd et al., 2021).
  • Evolving Crime Patterns: May struggle with emerging crime types and technologies (Weisburd et al., 2021).

5.0 Analysis Summary

E-mobility crime has emerged as a significant public safety concern in the UK, presenting considerable challenges for law enforcement. The primary issue identified is the absence of standardized registration and tracking systems for e-mobility vehicles such as e-bikes, e-scooters, and e-motorcycles. This gap renders these vehicles appealing for criminal and anti-social activities including theft, drug transportation, and reckless riding. Consequently, urban areas like London and the West Midlands have become hotspots for e-mobility crimes (PROFM Group, 2024), where the high prevalence of these vehicles and inadequate policing efforts hinder control over the situation. One of the main reasons these vehicles are so enticing to criminals is their speed, mobility, and the difficulty in tracing them, complicating both prevention and enforcement efforts. The comparative analysis of traditional vehicle crimes versus e-mobility crimes between 2021 and 2023 indicates that traditional vehicle crimes have decreased by roughly 15%, attributed to advancements in anti-theft technologies and heightened public awareness. Conversely, e-mobility crimes have increased by 45% during the same timeframe (Reed et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). This striking contrast is primarily due to the swift and largely unregulated adoption of e-mobility vehicles, leaving law enforcement ill-equipped to tackle the new challenges they pose. To effectively address these issues, it is essential to:

  • Implement national policies that require registration and licensing for all e-mobility vehicles.
  • Enhance training for law enforcement agencies to better manage e-mobility-related crimes.
  • Create public awareness campaigns to educate citizens on the legal use of these vehicles.
  • Furthermore, leveraging advanced technologies like AI-driven crime prediction models can assist law enforcement in identifying and anticipating potential hotspots while proactively addressing crime risks.

The SARA model, proposed by the AWS public safety team, offers a comprehensive and structured approach to combating e-mobility crime in the United Kingdom. Integrating this model into law enforcement practices could alleviate up to 60% of current criminal challenges, particularly when coupled with predictive analytics and improved data sharing. Despite challenges related to data dependence and the constantly evolving nature of crime, ongoing assessment and adaptation will ensure its sustained effectiveness. In conclusion, addressing e-mobility crime necessitates a holistic strategy that combines regulation, data integration, public education, and focused enforcement. This approach aligns with the introduction’s emphasis on coordinating efforts between law enforcement and policymakers to respond to the rising trend of e-mobility-related offenses in the UK.
